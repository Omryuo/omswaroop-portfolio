Most phishing triage starts the same way: a user forwards a "weird" email, and you have a couple of minutes to decide whether it's a nuisance or the start of an incident. The body and the display name lie freely. The headers are where the truth usually hides.

## Start with the authentication results

The single most useful line is `Authentication-Results`. It summarises three checks the receiving server already did for you:

- **SPF** — was the sending IP allowed to send for the envelope domain?
- **DKIM** — is the message cryptographically signed by the domain it claims?
- **DMARC** — does the visible `From:` align with SPF/DKIM, per the domain's policy?

A `dmarc=fail` on a message that claims to be from a bank is a strong signal. But the inverse trap matters more: **`dmarc=pass` does not mean safe.** Attackers register look-alike domains and pass authentication for *their own* domain. Authentication tells you the mail is genuinely from `paypa1-secure.com` — it doesn't tell you that domain isn't malicious.

## Walk the Received chain bottom-up

The `Received:` headers stack newest-on-top, so read from the bottom. The earliest hop shows where the message actually originated. Two things to look for:

1. A first hop that doesn't match the claimed sender's infrastructure.
2. A sudden jump through an unexpected country or a residential/cloud IP range.

## Check alignment, not just presence

The classic spoof passes SPF for a throwaway domain in the `Return-Path` while showing a trusted brand in the `From:`. Compare the envelope sender (`Return-Path`) against the visible `From:`. When they diverge and DMARC is only `p=none`, the message can still land in an inbox looking legitimate.

## Build the muscle memory

When you triage dozens of these, you stop reading headers linearly and start pattern-matching: auth results, then origin hop, then `From`/`Return-Path` alignment, then reply-to. The goal isn't to analyse every email forever — it's to reach a confident verdict in under a minute and escalate the few that deserve it.
